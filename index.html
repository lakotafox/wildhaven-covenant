The Mission

Most conservation money rents impact for a few years. The plan for Wildhaven is to buy permanence: land protected in perpetuity by enforceable legal instruments — not a grant cycle, not a campaign, but a promise built to outlive everyone who made it.

The land won't sit empty. Protected preserves are designed to double as release sites and lifelong sanctuary for wildlife that can't return to the wild. The animals get a home; the habitat gets defenders. That pairing is the whole idea — see the Covenant.

The Stewardship Covenant (who we intend to be)

Wildhaven is designed to be a boring, bulletproof secular nonprofit that doesn't hide that it has a soul. Everyone who joins will sign the Stewardship Covenant — the plain-language creed about what we owe the living world. An excerpt:

"We did not make the wild and we will not own it. We are not its masters but its stewards, and a steward's only power is restraint. We promise the land what almost no one promises it: not improvement, not use — permanence. We hold it so that what lives there may go on living, long after our names are forgotten."

The Endowment

Fee-Simple Ownership

The trust buys or receives land and owns it outright. Best for sanctuary and release sites — direct control of the habitat, able to host recovering animals. The trust's charter will forbid selling protected parcels, so the land never re-enters the market.

Conservation Easement

A landowner keeps and lives on their land but permanently gives up the right to develop or subdivide it. The trust holds that restriction and is legally bound to monitor and defend it in perpetuity, including against future owners.

The Stewardship Endowment

The part rookie land trusts get fatally wrong: protection costs money forever. The rule here will be simple — no parcel is accepted without a funded endowment for perpetual monitoring and legal defense. That is why the founding has to be done right first.
