The Plan

No preserves exist yet. No animals are in care yet. Here is exactly what Wildhaven will do, in what order, and what your founding contribution pays for first. Every line here is a plan, not a claim.

⚖ The Rules We'll Bind Ourselves To
Phase 0 — Form it right (now)

Incorporate as a nonprofit. Adopt bylaws and the Covenant. Seat an independent board (3+ unrelated people). Get an EIN. Decide the tax-deductibility path: secure a fiscal sponsor (an existing 501(c)(3) that receives deductible gifts on our behalf) and/or file IRS Form 1023. This is what founding contributions fund.

Phase 1 — Endow before we acquire

Stand up the stewardship-endowment policy and a legal-defense reserve. The rule is absolute: no parcel is accepted without funding to defend it forever. Register for charitable solicitation in the states where we'll fundraise.

Phase 2 — The first parcel

A single small parcel or one conservation easement — donated, bargain-sale, or purchased — with its endowment attached on day one. Proof of concept over scale.

Phase 3 — Pair land with animals

Once protected ground exists and is funded, build the rehab-and-release and lifelong-sanctuary capability on it. Land first, animals onto the land — never the reverse.

What "the first parcel" really means

We are not naming preserves we don't own. The first protected land will be chosen by one test: can we hold it and fund its defense forever, starting the day we accept it? If not, we don't take it — no matter how beautiful.

Candidates will most likely be: a donated marginal parcel, a bargain-sale from a conservation-minded owner, or a conservation easement on land someone wants to keep but never wants developed. When there is a real parcel, it will be named here with its real acreage, its real endowment status, and its real monitoring schedule — and not one day before.

If you own land (or know land) that should never be developed, that conversation can start now: land@wildhavencovenant.org.
  • Never sell protected land. Written into the charter and every deed.
  • No parcel without an endowment. Perpetuity has to be paid for.
  • Reverence in the open, structure underneath. Secular nonprofit; the belief is cultural, never a tax shield.
  • No private gain. Independent board, published finances, reasonable-comp policy.
  • Honest numbers only. Zero is reported as zero. Always.

These are spelled out in the Stewardship Covenant.

Why fund it before there's anything to see?

Because the difference between a real land trust and a sad story is whether it was formed correctly and funded to keep its promises. The unglamorous founding work — incorporation, the board, the endowment policy, the tax-deductibility path, state registration — is the part that makes "forever" true instead of a slogan. That is what a founding contribution buys. Not a logo. The spine.
